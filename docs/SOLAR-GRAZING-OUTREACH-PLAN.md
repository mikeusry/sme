*Created: 2026-03-04*

## Service Area

## Data Sources

### 1. EIA Form 860 (Primary)

US Energy Information Administration publishes a free downloadable spreadsheet of every utility-scale solar plant in the US — including owner name, operator, capacity, county, and coordinates.

- Download: https://www.eia.gov/electricity/data/eia860/
- Filter: **State = GA**, **Technology = Solar PV**
- Fields: plant name, owner/operator company, county, capacity (MW)

### 2. Georgia Power Interconnection Queue

Georgia Power publishes their interconnection queue — every solar project that's applied to connect to the grid, including ones under construction. Shows **future sites** before they need grazing.

### 3. County GIS / Tax Assessor

For sites visible on Google Maps/satellite:
- County tax assessor websites list parcel owners
- Many GA counties have free online GIS portals
- Parcel owner is often a project LLC — search that LLC to find the parent developer

### 4. ASGA Network

We are ASGA members. Ask the association:
- Do they maintain a list of solar operators seeking graziers?
- Can they make introductions?
- Are there RFPs or bid opportunities posted to members?

## Outreach Strategy

### Build the List

Create a spreadsheet with:
- Site name
- County
- Owner/operator company
- MW capacity
- Contact name & info
- Status (not contacted / letter sent / called / meeting / proposal / active contract)
- Follow-up date
- Notes

### Two-Touch Outreach

1. **Physical letter** — Professional, short, on Soul Miner's Eden letterhead
   - Include ASGA membership
   - Include a photo of sheep under panels
   - Emphasize proximity and local presence
   - Highlight: no chemicals, lower cost than mowing, better for panels (no rock damage)

2. **Follow-up call** — 5-7 days after letter
   - "I sent a letter last week about vegetation management..."
   - Physical mail stands out — solar O&M managers deal with physical sites and respect tangible outreach

### LinkedIn Targeting

Search: `[Company Name] + site manager OR operations manager OR vegetation management`

These are the people who decide on grazing contracts.

## Call Preparation Checklist

### Site Questions
- How many acres?
- Total graze or graze plus mow? Any chemical usage?
- How many sheep?
- Existing fencing & fencing requirements?
- Water onsite? Electricity?
- Year-round management or seasonal?

### Pay & Contract
- Pay structure and frequency?
- Per-acre rate vs. per-head vs. flat fee?
- Contract term — 1 year? Multi-year? Renewal terms?
- Performance metrics — height thresholds, inspection scores?
- Termination clause — notice period, early exit penalties?
- Exclusivity — can we take other solar grazing contracts nearby?

### Insurance & Liability
- Insurance requirements?
- Dogs allowed?
- Panel damage liability — if an animal damages a panel, who pays?
- Livestock loss liability — if equipment or site conditions injure/kill animals?

### Site Access & Logistics
- Panel height/type/spacing — can sheep move freely underneath?
- Trailer access — can livestock trailers reach the site? Road/gate conditions?
- Site security — locked gates, who has access?
- Target vegetation height specs from panel manufacturer or utility?
- Current vegetation — species mix, invasives?
- Terrain — flat, sloped, drainage issues?
- Has the site been sprayed recently? Residue risk?

### Animals & Operations
- Who provides the sheep? Us or them?
- Predator pressure — coyotes, stray dogs?
- Closest large-animal vet?
- Shelter for extreme weather?
- Who designs the grazing rotation?
- Off-season plan — where do animals go? Transport costs?

### Subcontractor-Specific
- What does the prime contractor handle vs. us? Clear scope split
- Reporting requirements — photos, logs, vegetation measurements?
- Site visit/update frequency expected?
- Other subs on site to coordinate with (mowing crews, electrical techs)?
- Their ASGA membership status — established or new to solar grazing?

### Transport & Costs
- Who covers transport costs?
- Who covers fencing setup/maintenance?
- If no on-site water, who pays for hauling?

## Key Selling Points

- **Proximity** — Based in Bogart, within 50 miles of most target sites
- **ASGA member** — American Solar Grazing Association
- **No chemicals** — Regenerative approach, no herbicide risk to panels or soil
- **No rock damage** — Mowers throw rocks at panels; sheep don't
- **Lower long-term cost** — Sheep maintain themselves between rotations
- **Dual benefit** — Vegetation management + soil health improvement

## Big-Picture Question

For each opportunity, determine early: **is this a subcontract or a partnership?**
- Subcontract = cheap labor with sheep, thin margins, 70-mile drives
- Partnership = manage the grazing program, better money, long-term relationship

Pursue partnerships. Walk away from bad subcontracts.
