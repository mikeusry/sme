## Executive Summary

Soul Miner's Eden started as a land restoration project inspired by the idea that we're called to steward what we've been given — to make it beautiful, productive, and a reflection of something greater than ourselves. That mission hasn't changed. What's changed is that the pieces are now in place to make the farm sustain itself financially while becoming more beautiful, not less.

Here's the core idea: we already sit on 5,000 tons of compost with zero competitors within 60 miles. That pile alone conservatively generates $60,000–$150,000 in revenue. It funds everything else. The livestock, the honor stand, the infrastructure — the compost bankrolls all of it without taking on debt or pulling from Southland operations.

We live 30 minutes from Athens — a town full of educated, high-income people who will pay $12 a dozen for pasture-raised eggs, $15 a gallon for raw milk, and $35 for a whole chicken. They don't blink at premium pricing because they're buying the story, not the commodity. They want to know where their food comes from, they want to visit the farm, and they want to post about it on Instagram. Our job is to make the farm worth posting about. That's it.

The beautiful part is that Priority #1 — making this property gorgeous — is the same thing that drives revenue. A beautiful farm with healthy animals on lush pasture surrounded by managed silvopasture is the marketing. It's the product proof for Southland's biology. It's the reason Athens families drive out on a Saturday to buy eggs and raw milk from an honor stand. And it's the backdrop for every piece of content we film for the Ag & Culture Podcast and YouTube channel.

Bo Carter brings animals, time, expertise, and a sawmill. We bring land, equipment, and staff. Together we're building a diversified small farm — cattle, sheep, goats, poultry, pigs — that operates on rotational grazing, silvopasture, and food forest principles. Everything is 100% natural, soy/corn-free, and transparent. The honor system farm stand is already built with power and county-approved traffic flow — it just needs coolers. The inspected kitchen enables value-add products like bone broth.

This is not a hobby farm. At base case projections, Year 1 revenue is $211,500 against ~$36,000 in startup costs. But it's also not an operation that sacrifices beauty for production. The priority stack is non-negotiable: Beautiful first. Easy to maintain second. Marketing value third. Financial returns fourth. If it doesn't make the property more beautiful, we don't do it.

The farm pays for itself. The land gets better every year. And the kids grow up watching it happen.

## 8. Food Forest (Zone I)

Bo's design follows the 6-layer food forest model: overstory → understory → shrub → vine → perennial → ground cover. Planted on contour swales for water retention. This zone transforms open/edge land into the most productive and beautiful area on the property.

### 8.1 Earthworks — Contour Swales

"It ain't how much rain you get, it's how much you keep."

- Use A-frame (or laser level) to find contour grade, then paint cut lines
- Mini excavator with ditching bucket — NOT a dozer (preserves existing grade)
- Swale dimensions: width = 2x depth (e.g., 36" wide x 18" deep)
- All dirt goes on the downhill side (berms)
- Connect swales so water cascades: top swale fills, overflows to next swale down
- Grass seed swale bottoms; plant berms with productive plants
- 2–3 swales through Zone I, following the natural contour

### 8.2 Food Forest Layers

| Layer | Species | Spacing/Notes |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| **Overstory (nut trees)** | Pecans, black walnuts (back near woods, away from fruit trees — juglone) | 40–60 ft spacing |
| **Understory (fruit trees)** | Apples, pears, peaches, mulberries, figs, persimmons, chestnuts | 20–30 ft spacing; for every 2 fruit trees, plant 1 nitrogen fixer between them |
| **Nitrogen fixers** | Redbud, ornamental honey locust, mimosa | Coppice every few years — sends exudates into soil to fertilize surrounding trees |
| **Shrub layer** | Blueberries, goji berries, blackberries, elderberry | 2 shrubs per fruit tree |
| **Vine layer** | Muscadines/scuppernongs — can run up fruit trees once established | Let them climb — they won't overtake mature trees |
| **Perennial layer** | Comfrey, thyme, mint, herbs, moringa (for livestock fodder — pelletizable, $1K pelletizer) | Between and around trees |
| **Ground cover** | Strawberries, clover, creeping thyme | 5 ground covers per shrub; plant on berms |

**Succession planting:** Mix early and late season flowering varieties across all layers for continuous harvest from spring through fall.

**Cedar tree removal:** Remove cedars from food forest area — antimicrobial properties interfere with fruit/nut production and soil biology. Also remove privet and callery pear (invasive).

### 8.3 Hugelkultur Berms

Use existing wood chip and mulch supply to build hugelkultur mounds for sweet potatoes and root crops:
- Lay logs/branches in trench
- Layer with sticks, dirt, wood chips, more dirt
- Slow decomposition builds soil over years
- Sweet potato mounds on top — easy harvest (no digging clay), vines edible as spinach substitute
- Potato beds: Kennebec variety (proven in this area), fingerlings in tubs/raised beds

### 8.4 Moringa

- Grows aggressively in Georgia summers (annual, dies back in winter)
- Livestock fodder (pelletizable with $1,000 pelletizer)
- Human consumption (leaves, powder)
- Fast-growing screen/beautification plant

## 10. Silvopasture Implementation

Silvopasture integrates trees, forage, and livestock on the same land — increasing per-acre productivity, improving animal welfare, building soil, and creating the aesthetic beauty that is Priority #1. The existing immature mixed forest and 20 acres of pasture provide a strong starting position.

### 10.1 Approach

Two parallel tracks based on existing land condition:

**Track 1 — Forest Thinning (Woodland → Silvopasture):** Selectively thin immature forest to 40–60 stems/acre, allowing sunlight to establish understory forage. Keep highest-value timber species (oak, hickory, walnut — the big oaks will look stunning standing alone). Remove pines, privet, callery pear, and cedars. Use pigs and goats for initial understory clearing before overseeding with shade-tolerant forages.

**Track 2 — Tree Establishment (Pasture → Silvopasture):** Plant tree rows in existing pasture at 30–40 ft spacing. Recommended species: black walnut (timber + nut income at year 15+), persimmon (wildlife + fruit), chestnut (feed supplement), and fast-growing natives for canopy. Protect young trees with electric fence or tree tubes. **Use the Case Vestrum 130 + power harrow + Compagna seeder for forage establishment between tree rows** — this is the Vestrum's highest-value use since it can't pull the turner.

**Eucalyptus:** Consider as understory planting on forest edges for fast growth and additional biomass. Research cold-hardy varieties for Zone 8a.

## 15. Agritourism Lodging — Zone K (Year 3–5 Build)

5 acres across the creek — naturally separated from farm operations by water. Guests feel immersed in the farm experience without interfering with daily work. The creek crossing itself becomes part of the arrival experience.

### 15.1 Strategic Position

The county currently allows STRs for resident property owners but is moving to restrict absentee/out-of-town operators. **This favors us.** As a resident owner operating a working farm with an established agritourism presence (honor stand, farm-to-table dinners, tours, workshops), we're the exact use case the county wants to protect. Getting permitted early, before restrictions tighten, locks in entitlement.

Athens/UGA creates a demand floor that most rural STRs don't have: 6–7 football Saturdays per year at $300–500+/night, graduation weekends, parents' weekends, and year-round Athens visitor traffic (restaurants, music, college tours). The rest of the calendar fills with agritourism guests — families wanting a farm experience, couples seeking a weekend escape, retreat groups.

### 15.2 Structure Options

| Option | Per-Unit Cost | Aesthetic | Build Time | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Tiny Homes (custom or prefab) | $40K–$80K | High — Instagram-ready, unique | 3–6 months | Most flexible. Can stage one as proof of concept. |
| Cottages (Bo's sawmill + rough-cut) | $60K–$120K | Highest — authentic, matches Ramsgate aesthetic | 6–12 months | Premium positioning, highest nightly rate. Bo's lumber reduces materials cost. |
| Yurts (platform + structure) | $15K–$30K | Unique, "glamping" tier | 1–3 months | Lowest barrier to entry. Test demand before building permanent structures. |
| Converted shipping containers | $30K–$50K | Modern/industrial contrast | 2–4 months | Durable, unique, lower cost than cottages |

**Recommended phasing:** Start with 1–2 yurts on platforms (low capital, fast deploy, test demand and pricing). If occupancy validates, build 2–3 cottages with Bo's rough-cut lumber for premium positioning. Total build-out: 4–6 units across 5 acres.

### 15.3 Revenue Model

| Scenario | Units | Avg Nightly Rate | Occupancy | Annual Revenue |
|----------|-------|-----------------|-----------|---------------|
| Phase 1 (2 yurts) | 2 | $175 | 40% | $51,100 |
| Phase 2 (+ 2 cottages) | 4 | $225 avg | 50% | $164,250 |
| Full Build (6 units) | 6 | $250 avg | 55% | $301,125 |

UGA game weekends alone (6–7 weekends x 2 nights x $400+/night x units) could generate $10K–$35K/year depending on unit count. That's before any normal occupancy.

### 15.4 Guest Experience Integration

This is where the farm flywheel kicks in. Every guest becomes a customer of the honor stand, a potential CSA subscriber, a social media post, and a word-of-mouth referral.

- **Welcome basket:** Eden eggs, raw milk, seasonal fruit, bone broth, Southland product samples
- **Self-guided farm tour:** QR code trail markers, each station tells the story (silvopasture, food forest, compost, livestock)
- **Honor stand access:** Guests walk across the creek, through the muscadine arbor, to the farm stand
- **Add-on experiences:** Farm-to-table dinner seats, guided tour with Bo, workshop enrollment, U-pick access
- **Content:** Every guest photographs and posts the property. The structures themselves need to be photogenic.

- Platform selection: Airbnb, VRBO, Hipcamp (for yurts), direct booking through soulminerseden.com

The 5-acre parcel across the creek is otherwise unproductive for farming (separated from operations, limited access). STR converts it into potentially the highest per-acre revenue on the entire property — $30K–$60K/acre/year at full build vs. $1K–$3K/acre for livestock grazing. The farm operations on the main property are what *create* the STR premium. Without the animals, the food forest, the honor stand, and the beauty — it's just another rural rental. With them, it's a destination.
